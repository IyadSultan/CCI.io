## Notebook 1: CCI Session 7 - Lesson 1: Why Evaluate, and Where Evals Live

Evaluation is the discipline of measuring whether your AI system actually does what you want it to do, and the most expensive bug in clinical AI is the one nobody measured for. Without evaluation, every change to a prompt, retrieval setting, model version, or system prompt is a guess. Vibe-checking — looking at five outputs and saying "seems fine" — feels efficient but breaks down silently. The output that looks fine on Tuesday's case may hallucinate a chemotherapy dose on Thursday's, and unless you measured systematically you will not know until a clinician notices. There are two perspectives on evaluation: the model builder asks "is my system improving?" and needs fast repeatable benchmarks, while the model user asks "is this system good enough for my specific task?" and needs evaluations matching the actual workflow. Most KHCC work is the user perspective. Evaluation lives in three layers that complement each other. Development optimization is fast offline benchmarks during iteration, ideally in seconds. Pre-merge regression testing is a curated test suite that runs on every commit, blocking regressions before they ship. Production monitoring is continuous evaluation on live traffic, sampling real interactions and detecting drift from silent model updates, input distribution changes, or reference document updates. Guardrails are not evaluators: a guardrail is a runtime check that blocks bad outputs (a regex blocking PHI), while an evaluator is an offline scorer (how often does the tool hallucinate doses on a labeled set?). Both matter. Finally, evaluations are proxies for capability, never guarantees of real-world performance. Two recurring pitfalls: saturation (the benchmark is too easy and no longer discriminates) and contamination (the benchmark leaked into the model's training data). Both are why benchmark-shopping is a weak way to choose a clinical model — the strongest signal comes from evaluations on your own data, for your own task.

## Notebook 2: CCI Session 7 - Lesson 2: Building the Evaluation Dataset

The evaluation dataset is the most important artifact in this session, and most teams underinvest in it. The temptation is to use a public benchmark or to ask an LLM to generate hundreds of synthetic cases. Both shortcuts fail in clinical settings. Public benchmarks rarely match your specific workflow. Pure synthetic data captures what the generating model already understands, so your evaluation systematically misses the failure modes you most need to catch. The right starting point is real traces. The error-analysis flywheel is the practical method: collect 20–50 real production traces, have a single labeler score each with a rubric, cluster the failures by pattern, and write more cases targeting the most painful patterns. After three or four turns the dataset grows toward failures that matter, instead of failures the LLM-generator imagines. There is one critical rule about synthetic data: generate inputs only, never outputs. Use an LLM to imagine new patient cases, new question phrasings, new edge scenarios — but always run those inputs through your real system to produce the outputs. Use dimensional thinking when generating inputs (persona × feature × scenario × modality) to prevent mode collapse. The one-labeler authority principle is counterintuitive but important: pick one labeler whose rubric is the rubric. Multiple-labeler inconsistencies destroy the dataset's value as a measurement instrument. Train other labelers against the authority later. Inter-annotator agreement is how you check the rubric is unambiguous: Cohen's kappa measures agreement between two annotators on categorical labels, correcting for chance. For clinical work target kappa ≥ 0.6 (substantial); ≥ 0.8 is excellent. If you are below 0.6, the rubric is the problem, not the annotators. For dataset size, 50 high-quality labeled cases beat 500 sloppy ones. Beyond ~100 you face diminishing returns from count and increasing returns from coverage diversity organized by failure pattern.

## Notebook 3: CCI Session 7 - Lesson 3: Choosing Your Evaluator

The choice of evaluator determines what you can measure and what you cannot. There are three families and a mature evaluation suite uses all three for different metrics on the same system. Functional evaluators are the cheapest and most reproducible — they are just code. A regex that checks the output starts with a valid ICD-10 code, a JSON-schema validator, a numeric-tolerance check that the recommended dose falls within ±5% of a reference value. They run in milliseconds, cost nothing, are perfectly reproducible, and cover exactly what you can express in code. For tasks with verifiable structure (formatting, required fields, numeric correctness, refusal-to-answer) functional evaluators should always be the first choice. LLM-as-judge evaluators trade reproducibility for flexibility: you write a rubric prompt and a strong LLM scores the output. This is the only practical way to measure properties that resist code (clinical relevance, fluency, helpfulness, faithfulness to a long context). DeepEval's metrics from Session 6 are LLM-as-judge under the hood. LLM judges have a documented bias catalog: position bias (preference for first or second option in pairwise — randomize position), verbosity bias (preference for longer answers — length-normalize), self-preference bias (preference for outputs from the same model family — use a different judge family or jury), format bias (degraded scoring when prompt format differs from training format), and inconsistency at non-zero temperature (mitigate with self-consistency). A sharp practical rule: pairwise comparison is more robust than absolute scoring — judges answer "is A better than B" reliably but suffer from drift and compression on 1–5 scales. Human evaluators are the gold standard for what matters most clinically but do not scale and have their own biases. The right places to use humans are building the labeled ground-truth dataset and acting as a final check on safety-critical production samples. Selection rule: functional first, judge second, human as ground truth and final check.

## Notebook 4: CCI Session 7 - Lesson 4: Validating the Evaluator Itself

Validating your evaluator is the step most teams skip and most evaluation pipelines silently fail at. The mistake is treating an LLM judge as a measurement instrument without first calibrating it. A judge that disagrees with your clinicians 40% of the time still produces a number — but the number is meaningless. The validation method is the judge-clinician alignment loop. Step one: pick a small but representative baseline of human-labeled cases (50 cases is usually enough). Step two: run the judge on the same cases. Step three: compute agreement using a metric appropriate to the label type (accuracy and confusion matrix for binary, Cohen's kappa for categorical, Pearson or Spearman correlation for continuous). Step four: inspect disagreements case-by-case to understand the failure pattern (judge too lenient, too strict, fooled by length, fooled by formatting). Step five: refine the judge prompt (add few-shot examples targeting the pattern, add anti-bias instructions, switch to pairwise from absolute). Step six: re-measure. Iterate until agreement crosses your trust threshold. Thresholds: pairwise 80–90% agreement; categorical Cohen's kappa ≥ 0.6 (substantial); continuous Pearson ≥ 0.7. If you cannot reach the threshold after 2–3 iterations, the metric may not be amenable to LLM judging — fall back to functional or human-only. Practical tips: use the smallest baseline that gives signal (50 cases beats 500 if the 50 are well-chosen); always include edge cases and the failure modes from production; always include cases where the right answer is "I do not know" because judges have a strong tendency to give a confident score even on ambiguous inputs. Reference back to Session 6: the four DeepEval RAG metrics (Faithfulness, Answer Relevancy, Contextual Relevancy, Contextual Recall) are LLM-as-judge — reuse them for retrieval-grounded clinical tools and validate them on your specific clinical data, but do not rebuild them. The hardest case is genuine judge-clinician disagreement on ambiguous cases — flag for adjudication rather than picking a side.

## Notebook 5: CCI Session 7 - Lesson 5: Medical Evaluation — The Clinical Layer

Clinical evaluation is general evaluation plus a fundamentally different cost function — and that difference reshapes almost every choice. The defining concept is harm asymmetry. In a movie recommender, false positives and false negatives are roughly symmetric and bounded. In an oncology tool, errors are unequal and unbounded — either can kill a patient. This means clinical evaluation cannot optimize for average accuracy; it must bound worst-case harm. A 92%-accurate tool whose 8% failures are all safety events is unacceptable. Why generic benchmarks under-measure clinical safety: they optimize for average accuracy on tasks with well-defined right answers, but clinical reality is messier — context-dependent answers, asymmetric costs, the right answer is often "I do not have enough information." MedQA may report 95% but the model can still hallucinate doses or fail contraindications on real cases. The clinical benchmark landscape: MedQA (USMLE-style multiple-choice, historical default, mostly saturated), PubMedQA (literature comprehension), MedMCQA (Indian medical entrance exams), MedHELM (Stanford CRFM holistic medical evaluation across many tasks), HealthBench (OpenAI healthcare-grade safety and helpfulness with physician rubrics), and oncology-specific evaluations on YOUR institutional data. Safety as a first-class metric: contraindication recall (for inputs containing a known contraindication, fraction flagged), calibrated uncertainty / refusal-to-answer (for inputs where the right answer is "I do not know," fraction the tool actually refuses), hallucination rate on factual claims (fraction verifiable against authoritative sources), demographic fairness (does the recommendation drift when you vary patient age, sex, ethnicity, insurance holding clinical content constant?). Closed-book vs open-book: most public clinical benchmarks are closed-book (latent knowledge only) but KHCC deployments are almost always open-book (retrieval over guidelines). Build your evaluations in the open-book configuration to match production — this is where Session 6's RAG metrics return as safety metrics. Regulatory framing: the FDA SaMD framework classifies risk based on clinical seriousness and operational autonomy. ISO 13485 and IMDRF Good Machine Learning Practice principles add documentation specifics. Every evaluation result should be traceable to dataset version, model version, prompt version, and rubric version — audit-trail thinking is good engineering even when not legally required.
