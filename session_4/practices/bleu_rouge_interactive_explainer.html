BLEU — Bilingual Evaluation Understudy

Proposed by Papineni et al. (2002) at IBM for machine translation. Measures how many n-grams in the candidate appear in the reference — it's precision-focused.

HOW IT WORKS

Counts overlapping n-grams (1-gram through 4-gram) between candidate and reference, then takes a geometric mean. A brevity penalty punishes overly short outputs.

STRENGTHS & LIMITS

Fast, reproducible, language-agnostic. But ignores recall, synonym variation, and sentence structure. A score of 1.0 = perfect; 0.6+ is generally good.

Precision-focused N-grams 1–4 Brevity penalty Range: 0–1 Lower = worse
ROUGE — Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation

Proposed by Chin-Yew Lin (2004) at USC for summarization. Measures how much of the reference appears in the candidate — it's recall-focused.

ROUGE-1 & ROUGE-2

ROUGE-1 overlaps individual words. ROUGE-2 overlaps bigrams. Both report Precision, Recall, and F1 — giving a fuller picture than BLEU alone.

ROUGE-L

Uses the Longest Common Subsequence (LCS) — words don't need to be adjacent. Captures sentence-level structure better than n-gram counting.

Recall-focused Unigram, bigram, LCS F1 score Range: 0–1 Summarization standard
BLEU vs ROUGE — when to use what?

USE BLEU WHEN

Evaluating machine translation or text generation where you care about fluency. You want to know: did the model produce the right words?

USE ROUGE WHEN

Evaluating summarization or clinical note extraction where coverage matters. You want to know: did the model miss anything important?

CLINICAL AI NOTE
In oncology NLP (e.g., extracting diagnosis from notes), ROUGE-recall matters more — missing a cancer diagnosis is worse than a slightly awkward phrasing.
